Term - P(Y|T)
|------------------------------>
 The ordinary conditional distribution, the population filtered down to the
 cases where T took a specific calue and sees what Y looks like in that subpop.

 T may have not been assigned to a random pop due to various reasons including
 a self assigned trial, this confounding can unknowningly mask the real cause
 of the observed outcome.
  - if the T="takes a specific vitamin", and Y is "Overall Health Outcome". The
    corelation between people that take supplements that are overall health
    concious individuals is high, so the results of the study may appear to
    indicate a larger treatment effect than it otherwise would be.

Term - P(Y|do(T))
|------------------------------>
 What would Y look like if we FORCED T to take a certain value uniformly across
 the pop. i.e. what would the treatment effect look like if we mandated that
 everyone take the vitamin supplement.

 do(T=t) means that you take the causal graph and cut all arrows pointing into
 T and set T=t by flat. Then you ask what distribution Y has under this
 modified system.


Term - Diffeomorphic Transformation
|------------------------------>
 Mapping between spaces that is:
   - Bijective (on to one and onto)
   - Smooth (infinitely differentiable), both the transformation and its
     inverse have no kinks, jumps or tears.
   - its inverse is also smooth - the inverse map is also smooth
   - T-1(T(x)) = x - it is has an inverse.

Term - Cumulative Distribution Function
|------------------------------>
 "What is the probability that my random varaible is <= some value x?"

 Formal Notation= FY(x) = P(Y <= x)
                  -----------------
 FY(-inf)=0 - nothing is below -inf
 FY(inf)=1 - everythin is below inf
 FY(x) - non decreasing - as x grows you only gain more probability.

 6-sided die
 FY(1) = P(Y<=1) = 1/6
 FY(3) = P(Y<=3) = 3/6 = 0.5
 FY(6) = P(Y<-6) = 6/6 = 1.0

Quotes:
=======

"For example, when validating an inference method which estimates an ATE under
certain confounding assumptions, it is crucial that generated data follow the
“ground truth” ATE and confounding assumptions one wishes to measure. However,
synthetic data risk being oversimplified and contrived, failing to reflect the
complexity of real world datasets"

"To mitigate this, generative models are trained on real-world data and
calibrated to generate samples with modifiable causal constraints. Such
constraints include the average causal treatment effect, unobserved
confounding, and positivity. To our knowledge, the FF framework proposed in
this paper is the first method to allow all of these conditions to adjusted by
the user. Existing methods (Neal et al., 2020; Athey et al., 2021; Parikh et
al., 2022) encode these effects through soft optimisation constraints, hence
there is no guarantee that the constraints are satisfied. Enforcing these
constraints too strongly may negatively impact model optimisation, and may
affect the reconstructive ability of the underlying model. Furthermore, since
these approaches do not explicitly parameterise the causal effect, samples from
trained models must be tested post hoc to ensure the desired constraints are
present in the sampled data. A key benefit of frugal models is that the
marginal causal effect is directly parameterised by the user through the
likelihood. As a result, synthetic data samples will exactly satisfy these
constraints."
